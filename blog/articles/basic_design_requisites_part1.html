The Five basic Design Requisites - Part I

- from Layout (1947)
by Charles J. Felten

Success in design is achieved mainly by establishing (tạo dựng, thiết lập) in the mind a diversified (đa dạng) vocabulary of sound (vững) design formulae (công thức), effective construction patterns (mẫu, hình, khung), and successfully tried procedures.

A square layout, wherein two square display units divide the area monotonously into 4 elements of the same dimension (kích thước, khổ). It is then re-designed into four vertical layouts that have height & width which are non-mathematically related, creating propotionately pleasing areas. As a result, the white space is transformed into vibrant areas. In the three thumbnails (hình đại diện thu nhỏ) at lower-right, the same procedure as used above is applied horizontally.

The five basic requisites (yếu tố cần thiết) are proportion, balance, contrast, rhythm, and unity. Their fuctions in design are predicated (căn cứ vào) on the basic principle that certain harmonious (hài hòa) combinations of shapes, masses, and tone create favourable (dễ chịu) impression on the mind. On the other hand, groups of unrelated & unharmonious elements produce unpleasant reactions.

One should acquire (học được, đạt được) a sound (vững vàng) fundamental understanding of the basic purpose of each design requisite, and the principles which govern (chi phối) its application.

How to position a display unit correctly & how to regulate (điều chỉnh) its size, shape, and tone effectively depend on a sense of correctness & good taste developed by experience, rather than by strict adherence (bám vào một cách cứng nhắc) to mechanical rules & measurements.

The proportions of the gray mass are pleasing & interesting, but the upper areas that it occupies (chiếm) are exactly the same as the white space below it. The eye quickly perceive the obvious relationship. By reducing the height of the mass, a better, non-mathematical division (sự phân chia) of space is attained (đạt được). The mass, however, loses some of its effectiveness. In the third sample, the mass becomes more dramatic, but now nearly square. The last example portrays the gray mass in a more vertical shape, to dominate (chiếm ưu thế) the layout area forcefully.

PROPORTION

An object is said to be well-proportion, if its shape is optically (thuộc về thị giác) interesting, its structural (mang tính kết cấu) parts harmoniously related yet not monotonous (đều đều, nhàm chán) in size, and the whole a combination of the artistic & the useful.

In nature, there is much evidence of such structural harmony. Trees and plants, stars and planets, the human body itself - all are examples of good proportion.

Being influenced by a naturally developed sense of correct proportion, the eye reacts favorably to designs that are well-proportioned, and rejects those which have mathematically obvious dimension, or which place together unrelated sizes and shapes.

In an absolute square, the four equal sides maybe said to be harmoniously related, but the area is static (tĩnh), monotonous, and unniteresting because the eye quickly perceives the equality (sự đồng đều) of dimensions.

Below are paired examples of how static areas & monotonous space divisions maybe improved in interest by changing mathematically obvious dimensions.

The uninteresting square at top becomes a better proportioned & more pleasing area by reducing its height, as shown at its right.

The below pairings beneath demonstrate how both areas & space pattern (kiểu, mẫu hình) within areas maybe improved by altering (thay đổi) dimensions, so that the eyes does not readily perceive their mathematical relationship.

Two ways of improving a double square layout.

These four pairings show basic examples of how proportion is improved in horizontal patterns by interesting rectangular & diagonal divisions.

How angular & circular areas maybe more interestingly proportioned and divided.

"It is obvious then, that the basic rule for attaining correct proportion is to regulate the dimensions of the area or objects so that the eyes does not readily fathom (hiểu, kết luận, nhận ra) the mathematical relation of its measurement."

When outside dimensions of areas cannot be altered (thay đổi), the areas within maybe made more interesting & activated (kích hoạt) by utilizing (sử dụng) borders (lề), panels (bảng), rules (đường kẻ), ornamentation (họa tiết trang trí), and other design media to achieve better proportion of white spaces.

BALANCE

Balance is one of Nature's most fundamental laws. It is defined as repose (sự tĩnh tại, sự phối hợp hài hòa), resulting from the equilibrium (sự cân bằng) of equal weights, or of unequal weights placed in properly balanced position.

In early childhood we learn the significance (tầm quan trọng) of attaining (đạt được) balance in our movements. And as we subconsciously (một cách vô thức) observe the proper distribution (sự phân bố) of weight in the objects of nature, we gradually develop a sense of optical balance. It is logical then, that we prefer layout compositions that are harmoniously balanced, rather than those that are not.

In design, balance is attained by carefully regulating the size, shape, tone, and position of the units in relation to the optical center of the area.

To be optically balanced, a composition of units need not be of a formal or centered arrangement, but units and masses should be so positioned that the entire composition appears naturally balanced in the design area, with the optical center as pivot (điểm chốt).

The optical center should be used. Because if a composition evolves (phát triển từ) from the exact mathematical center, the composition will always appear low to the eye, and seem unbalanced vertically.

The cross in the first example shows the approximate position of the optical center, somewhat above the mathematical center. The next example places an element approximately in the optical center, while the third illustrates that an elements apperar too low & unbalanced vertically when in exact center.

The basic principle for maintaining balance in a layout can best be illustrated by the following example: Assume that two rectangular units of equal size & tone are balanced on the central pivot (điểm chốt chính giữa). 

  • If one is enlarged in size, its tonal weight must be increased to maintain balance.
  • If either is moved to right or left, its size or tone must be regulated according to its relative position (vị trí tương đối) from the pivotal point.

"Thus, fundamentally, by the control of size, tone, and position of units, balance is regulated."

The shape of units, also, must be considered. Because irregularly shaped (có hình kì dị) units such as silhouettes, vignettes, etc. exert (sử dụng, dùng, mang lại) more influence on the eye than do rectangular units, therefore maybe said to be of greater weight. Correctly balanced designs stem (mọc ra, phát ra) mainly from a sense of balance, developed by constant (liên tục) study & application, rather than from rigid (cứng nhắc) rules.

These sketches illustrate the principle that when elements are unequal in size, optical balance must be maintained (bảo toàn) by regulating shape, tone, and position of elements in relation to the optical center.

Circular shapes (có hình tròn) exert (sử dụng, dùng, mang lại) more attraction than do similarly toned rectangular shapes of approximate size; likewise, irregularly shaped objects attract the eye more than either, and must be skillfully placed to maintain balance.

CONTRAST

Contrast is the quality in design that imparts (truyền tải) life, sparkle, variety, and emphasis to a composition. It eliminates (xóa bỏ) monotony and accentuate (nâng cao) display values. As similarity of dimensions is to be avoided to attain correct proportion, so is monotonous similarity in size, shape, tone, and direction of units inappropriate to good contrast.

"Proper contrast is achieved by subordinating (dìm, hạ thấp) inconsequential (vụn vặt) elements, and giving display emphasis to the more important units."

Here two display elements of monotonous alikeness in size, shape, and tone are uninteresting. Examples at top show how contrast is achieved by emphatically (dứt khoát, rõ ràng) enlarging one element, and reducing the tone value of the other. The bottom examples illustrate the same basic principle, and also how their overlapping increases contrast & create unity.

While monotony of height & width of display units should be avoided, variety of sizes & shapes must be limited. Because overemphatic (nhấn mạnh quá đà) contrast defeats its purpose, and results in a spotty (loang lổ), discordant (bất hòa, nghịch tai) design.

Contrast in shape should be achieved without effecting (tạo ra, gây ra) too many unrelated, irregularly shaped elements. Regtangular masses should predominate (chiếm ưu thế) over odd shapes like silhouettes, vignettes, ovals, and circles, which should be used sparingly (sơ sài, dè xẻn, sương sương).

Black & white creates the sharpest tonal contrast. But the most interesting compositions are those that contain these two extremes in combination with limited intermediary gray tones.

As in the shaping (nặn, gọn, định hình) of elements, contrast in tone likewise must be carefully controlled. Because too many tonal values will cause a spotty, jumpy appearance, which confuses the eye & divert (đánh lạc hướng) attention incorrectly.

In the right layout, utmost (tột đỉnh, nhất có thể) contrast has been created by emphasizing size, shape, tone, and position of the predominating element.

Contrast in direction is achieved by regulating the shape of units & masses. The eye follows the vertical shape downward, a horizontal mass to left or right, a diagonal line in the direction of its slope, and an irregular shape to the point of interest.

"Therefore, contrast in shape can be utilized to carry the eye in the desired direction, to visualize all elements in their proper order."

Slow movement in informal arrangement is exemplified (được minh họa, ví dụ) in the layouts titled Flower Show, Fishing, and Pattern at the upper-right. Faster-moving examples are the sketches titled Flavor, Gracious Living, and Parkay at the upper-left. In the Interior sketch in the lower-right, action is simulated (bắt chước, dựa theo) by the directional movement of the products. 

Informal designs stem basically from lively, interesting space divisions & ingenious (khéo léo), contrasting patterns. Movement maybe slow, fast, or of an intermediate tempo (tốc độ vừa phải). It depends on the design pattern, the directional (có hướng) shapes of the elements, and their arrangement.
