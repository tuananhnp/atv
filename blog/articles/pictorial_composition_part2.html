Lines, Forms in Pictures

- from A New Approach to Pictorial Composition (1941)
by Harve B. Wobbe

This article discusses the effects of lines, forms, textures and patterns within pictures. Ultimately, it answers the fundamental questions of how to utilize & regulate those elements to convey movements, emotion, mood, and other certain visual impact upon beholders.

LINES & THEIR EMOTIONS

The direction of lines create motion within a picture. They help to convey the emotion & mood of the picture.

  • Vertical lines are symbolic of dignity, stateliness, and grandeur.
  • Horizontal lines express peace, tranquility, solitude, and repose.
  • Bent & slanted (diagonal) lines sugguest action.

Besides these emotions, the type of line (horizontal or vertial) gives a feeling of vastness in the direction of the flow of line.

As our eye sees the direction & flow of the predominating lines in a picture, we sense its mood. If the lines are vertical, we inadvertently know that the theme is one of dignity, stateliness, and grandeur. The predominating verticals lift us in the picture, and we are impressed with height & limitless space above us. It is just as we look at a tall church spire, a towering pine, or a skyscraper.

On the other hand, when there are predominating horizontal lines, the feeling is one of peacefulness, tranquility, or repose. A path winding across the picture, the plowman in furrowed field moving across the picture make us conscious of the breadth & expanse of the land.

 Action is pictures can only suggested. The principal way of suggesting action is with bent or slanting lines. These lines show the direction of the action in the picture. For instance, when we see that several trees are bent over, we sense the presence of wind in the landscape, and even know which it is blowing. The action suggested in the bent form of the athlete is confined to the figure.

While the majority of action in pictures will be suggested by bent lines, as stated above, there are also several ways to show that objects are in motion. For example: a picture of a girl in a car with her hair flowing out behind her tells us immediately that the car is moving. If her hair were falling naturally over her shoulder we would know that the car was not in motion.

Unattached or unsupported objects sometimes imply action. An illustration of this is a figure in the air such as a dander or diver to which motion is given by its position. An unattached leaf in the air is obviously falling or being blown by the wind.

DETERMINATION OF SHAPE OF PICTURE

The question is often raised as to whether a picture should be made as horizontal or vertical rectangle, a square, oval, or circle. The majority of pictures fit well in either a vertical or horizontal rectangle.

Portraits of women & children are often greatly improved by making them into an oval or circle. The lines of these forms harmonize with these subjects, but would be inappropriate for the strength & ruggedness of a man's portrait. Still life, design, and decorative pictures may also be printed as circles or ovals. Occasionally, a landscape may be made into an oval, circle, or square.

The best guide to follow when choosing the form of the picture is to use the one which harmonizes with the predominating lines or forms within the picture. When in doubt, it is wise to remember that a rectangle is alway acceptable.

FORMS WITHIN PICTURES

The masses, lines, and objcts within a picture may fit into, or form, any one of several fundamental shapes. These are:

  • Triangular
  • Circular
  • "S" curve
  • "L" shape
  • Cross
  • Radiation
  • Diagonal

Triangular composition is found when the object or a group of objects in the picture fit into a triangular form

The figure in this picture will fit into a triangle. The triangular form is repeated by the bent arm & legs.

Triangular composition is also found when three objects, all vital to our picture, form a triangle when they are connected with imaginary lines.

In this picture, our eye travels from one figure to another, and back to the first figure again. No matter which figure is seen first, the same path is followed & the route is triangular.

When an object or a group of objects form or fit into a circular form, we have circular composition.

Here is a picture which illustrates circular composition. The tree trunks, the branches, and the shadows on the ground enfold a circular area.

When our eye travels from on object to another, and so on, in a circular path, we have another type of circular composition.

The starting point is the girl in the boat. From here the eye travels to the left along the boat to the bushes at the base of the tree, then up the tree trunk, then follows the branches to the right. The hanging branches take us back to the figure in the boat. We have traveled through the picture area in a circle.

The "S" curve is one of the most pleasing forms of composition. It gives a graceful & restful feeling to the picture. Our eye follows its line through the picture in a smooth, un-hurried movement. As the name implies, this type of composition is present when an "S" curve is the predominating form in the picture.

The "S" curve type of composition is applied this photo. Our eye travels easily through the picture along the curving, snow covered banks of the stream.

The "S" curve type of composition may also be formed by mentally connecting points. This figure shows dotted lines connecting three points to form an "S" curve.

This "S" curve is shown photographically in this picture, where the bases of the trees when connected form an "S".

There are no curves in this picture, because the picture is made of only straight lines. However, this has the "S" curve present. The stairs change direction, and when the eye follows them it travels over an "S" curve.
