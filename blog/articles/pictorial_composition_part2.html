The Eye is Attracted to Light

- from A New Approach to Pictorial Composition (1941)
by Harve B. Wobbe

The word photography defines itself for "photo" means light, and "graphy" means writing. Photography then is writing with light. And a photograph is a picture made by light of various intensities.

Our interest is in the breaking waves & the water churning (đánh tung) over the rocks. It is true that our eye is attracted to the tiny white sail (thuyền buồm) on the horizon, but this serves further to accent (nhấn mạnh) the foreground (tiền cảnh) by comparison (sự so sánh) with the surging (dâng, dấy lên) power of the ocean. The picture has been portioned (chia tỉ lệ) so that there is only one-quarterd sky area. This gives deserved prominence (sự nổi bật) to the foreground.

Light is perhaps the most dominating (vượt trội) factor (nhân tố) in our lives. Light brightens (chiếu sáng) our surroundings (cảnh xung quanh), while the absence (sự vắng mặt) of light makes them drab (buồn tẻ, xám xịt). Photography is dependent upon light. Just as an artist uses pigments to paint his pictures, so the photographer paints & model (tạo hình) his pictures with light.

The proper use of light to give feeling & atmosphere is of utmost (tột đỉnh) importance. Light accents objects and areas. These accents are highly desirable (đáng mong muốn) when correctly placed, but it detracts (ngược với attract) from the picture when they are not.

Pictures are closely allied (cùng tính chất) to music. Music is a harmonious combination & arrangement of tones. There are a limited number of notes in music. The value of these notes & their arrangement makes the music.

So it is with a picture. There are a limited number of tones which can be likened (giống như là) to the notes of music. They may vary in size & shape as round, square, or triangular; and may also vary in tone value from black through the different grays to white.

Thus, where music is an arrangement of notes of various durations (độ dài, thời lượng) which form (hình thành) chords (hợp âm), a picture is the arrangement of masses (khối), lines, and tones. Pictures, like music, may or may not be pleasing depending upon the arrangement.

If a musician were to play only one note, it would soon become monotonous, and we would lose interest in his efforts. Likewise, when we look at an area of a single tone, there is no interest.

Here there is no point demanding attention, no movement, just a static uninteresting area. The eye wanders (lang thang, lạc đường) over this area & sees nothing to arrest (nắm bắt) it, then moves to more interesting places.

If the musician were to add several notes and make a sequence (chuỗi), it would gain interest because of its movement. Here is a picture of several tones. Just as our ears respond to notes of different tones, so our eyes respond to areas of different tones. Movement is created in a picture by the presence of several tones.

Generally, the eye is more attracted to areas of light tones than those of dark tone. As we look at the picture above, our eye will come to rest within the white area, eventhough it starts at one of darker tones. For example, if we start by looking at the black area, we next look at the gray, the lighter gray, and then the white.

The movement is from the bottom toward the top. If we turn the picture upside down, the eye travels from top to bottom. We might also cause the movement to be from right to left, or from left to right, by shifting (đổi chỗ) the position of the diagram.

In the above picture, we have written the profile (nét mặt chụp nghiêng) of a girl with light. Our eye draws the picture for us by traveling over the light outline. Obviously, the white outline in itself does not make the picture. Other tones are needed to make it an outline, and to accentuate (nhấn mạnh) its direction.

In this illustration, the eye may be attracted first to the dark because of its outline & contrast, but it will not remain (stay) there for (because) it will wander out of the picture area via (through) any edge (mép, viền).

Now the tones have been reversed (lật ngược). The white area catches and holds the eye. However, this area is centered mathematically. It is not good practice to have interest directed to the exact center of the picture.

By moving the white area away, the result is less disturbing. When the eye rests on the white area which is placed away from the exact center of the picture, the static effect of the previous picture is overcome.

The correct placement of the center of interest has been accepted as one of four different places, but each has the same mathematical relationship to the area. To find this correct placement, divide the picture into thirds, both vertically & horizontally. The four points where the division lines meet are the positions of the center of interest.

This figure shows the divisions of the picture area by lines.

These four points indicate (chỉ ra) the exact location of the center of interest. It is not necessary that the center of interest be placed precisely at one of these points.

We have the boat, the most important object, in the center of the picture. This spaces (chia tách) the rest of the picture quite evenly, and makes it difficult to devote (dâng, cho dành cho) attention to either the foreground or background.

This shot & the next one have been composed so that the boat is not in the center of the picture, but is placed where it should be according to the rule of thirds. Here the boat in high placement (sự đặt, vị trí) gives prominence (sự nổi bật) to the foreground, with its floating leaves.

By placing the boat in the lower interest point, our eye travels into the distance (phía xa) instead of staying in the foreground.

DIVISION OF AREA

The total area of a picture must often be divided & allocated (chia & phân bổ) to its various parts. There can, of course, be but one area of a picture which is predominant. Areas of equal size vie (ganh đua, đánh nhau) for attention. The size of the different areas is governed (chi phối) by their importance in the picture. The maker must therefore decide upon the theme of his picture, and what he wants it to stress, then divide it accordingly.

"Pictures should not be divided into equal areas."

This figure has the same amount of light area & dark area, which we can liken (cho là giống) to the land & sky. This division would make it hard for the viewer to give complete attention to the area which tells the story.

These two figures have the area divided so that there is one-thirds land & two-thirds sky, and two-thirds land & one-thirds sky. This is an application of the rule of thirds as applied to areas. Approximate (xấp xỉ, gần đúng) application of this rule is sufficient, as there are times when more or less of each area is desireable.

The division of area in this picture makes it hard to center interest in either areas.

Now we have the large area of sky. We are more interested in the clouds, and have a feeling of sunshine & the out-of-doors generally.

Our interest is in the foreground, with its field of tobacco & the wagon loaded with cut tobacco, which is being taken to the drying sheds (lều, lán, chuồng). In this instance, the picture tells a better story by being divided so that the foreground is the larger area.

As previously explained, the subject matter (cách chọn vật thể) of the above illustration was best suited to a division of area with the land portion (phần được chia) predominating. Another picture might better be divided so that the sky area predominates. The prospective (lường trước, sắp xảy ra) picture should be studied carefully so that the area is divided correctly.

The picture below was taken with a large sky area, because it was felt that the final interest was in the kites sailing in the clouds. It might be argued (phản bác), and with some justification (biện hộ), that the people are of greater interest than kites. This is true, but when we do not know the people in the picture, we are more interested in what they are doing. Except, of course, in the case of a fine charater study.

Therefore, as the interest of the boys is definitely directed to the kites, our interest follows theirs. We also end up in the clouds with them, and recall our own kite flying days. The importance of the figures would have been minimized by having them in less than one-third of the picture area.

"It is not desirable to have a parallel area cut across the picture, especially so that the mass divides the area into equal parts."

In this picture, the dark mass divides the area into equal parts above & below.

These two figures show the improvement when these same areas do not divide the picture evenly.

In the above illustration, the dark mass composed (gồm có) of boats, buildings, and their reflections (sự phản chiếu), is so placed in the picture area as to give the same amount of sky & water.

This picture has been taken so that the dark mass does not cut through the center of the picture.

Compared to the previous example, the subject matter is more suitable to the division of area, which makes it possible to include the whole of the reflected images. And since the boats are the subject of the picture, it is more appropriate to include a larger area of water than sky.

Just as a single area should not cut across the picture, it is also undesirable for several parallel areas to cross the picture. For example, the below picture appears to be "step like". The movement is abrupt (đột ngột, cộc lốc) & unpleasant, as we climb from the water to the row (hàng) of rocks, to the water, over the dam (con đập), and water to the shore line.

When the picture is taken from a different viewpoint, so that a vertical line opposes (đối nghịch lại) these parallel, the picture is more interesting.
